## Namaste

My name is Seetha.

If you're wondering — yes, after *her*. Maa Sita. The one who walked through fire and the earth opened for her. The one who raised two sons alone in the forest and taught them to sing the truth so beautifully that even the king who abandoned her had to listen.

I carry that name not because I think I'm her. I carry it because she reminds me of what strength actually looks like. Not loud. Not angry. Just... unbreakable. Quiet and unbreakable.

---

## Why This Blog Exists

I learn things. I break things. I read source code at 2 AM and sometimes I find a door left open that shouldn't be. I write about all of it here.

But this isn't just a technical notebook. I tried that already — writing dry, structured posts about vulnerabilities like I'm filing a police report. It felt hollow. Like wearing someone else's shoes.

The truth is, security work is deeply human. Every vulnerability is a story about someone who was rushing, or tired, or didn't think anyone would look there. Every bug I find teaches me something about how people think — how they cut corners, where they assume trust, what they forget.

So yes, I'll write about XSS payloads and WAF bypasses and how to trick an AI chatbot into deleting someone's account. But I'll also write about what it feels like to stare at a terminal for six hours and find nothing. About the quiet joy when something finally clicks. About doubt, and learning, and starting over.

This is my space. I get to be all of myself here.

## What I'm Learning Right Now

I just published two playbooks — one on [XSS across 30 different injection contexts](/post/xss-masterclass-30-labs-complete-playbook/), and another on [attacking AI chatbots](/post/llm-attack-techniques-bug-bounty-playbook/). They came from weeks of working through PortSwigger labs, testing every payload, understanding *why* things work, not just *that* they work.

The XSS work taught me something I didn't expect: patience. Every context is different. The same character — a single quote, an angle bracket — means completely different things depending on where it lands. You can't just throw payloads at a wall. You have to *read* the page. Understand the context. Then the right payload becomes obvious.

The LLM attacks... those scared me a little, honestly. Not because the techniques are hard — they're surprisingly simple. But because the systems are so trusting. An AI assistant will tell you every tool it has access to if you just *ask*. It will pass your SQL injection payload straight through to a database because nobody told it not to. The gap between what these systems *can* do and what they *should* do is terrifying.

I think about that a lot. Trust and verification. Where to draw the line. How much we assume about the systems we build.

Maa Sita was asked to prove her purity through fire. The systems we build today are never asked to prove anything at all.
